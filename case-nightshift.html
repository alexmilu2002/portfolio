NightShiftA tangible habit tracker.

Duration8 weeks TypeUX design & prototyping ToolsArduino, laser cutter
Prototype, electronics, and storyboard. Fig. 03

NightShift was an eight-week group project in which we designed and developed an interactive product aimed at helping people with ADHD establish a healthier and more consistent bedtime routine. The project combined user research, UX design, physical prototyping, and hardware development, allowing us to work through the entire design process from identifying a problem to creating a functional prototype.

Our project began with extensive user research to better understand the challenges people with ADHD experience when preparing for sleep. Through interviews, desk research, and user analysis, we discovered that many individuals struggle with racing thoughts and unfinished tasks at the end of the day, making it difficult to relax before going to bed. Based on these findings, we defined our target audience and formulated a design challenge focused on reducing mental clutter and encouraging users to end their day in a calm and structured way.

The result was NightShift, an interactive habit-tracking product that transforms completing daily tasks into a satisfying bedtime ritual. Throughout the day, users can complete their planned activities, and once a task is finished, they simply press a button on a wireless remote. Every completed task activates an additional light in the product, gradually illuminating a city skyline. This creates a clear visual overview of the user's progress while providing a rewarding sense of accomplishment. By turning task completion into an engaging and motivating experience, the product encourages users to build a consistent bedtime routine while reducing the stress of unfinished tasks.

A key aspect of the project was the integration of gamification principles. Instead of displaying a traditional checklist, NightShift rewards users with visual progress, making everyday routines feel more enjoyable and motivating. The calming lighting effects and gradual transformation of the city skyline provide positive reinforcement without overwhelming the user, supporting both productivity and relaxation.

Throughout the project, my team and I followed a user-centered design process that involved continuous research, brainstorming, ideation, testing, and iteration. We explored a wide variety of creative design methods to generate ideas and better understand our users' needs. These methods included empathy mapping, customer journey mapping, affinity mapping, morphological charts, "How Might We" questions, and other creative brainstorming techniques such as tarot cards of tech and the Four Actions Framework. By testing and evaluating different concepts early in the process, we were able to refine our ideas and make design decisions based on user feedback rather than assumptions.

Once the concept had been validated, we moved on to creating both digital and physical prototypes. We built several low- and high-fidelity prototypes to test the interaction, usability, and overall experience before developing the final product. A significant part of the physical prototype was produced using a laser cutter, allowing us to precisely manufacture custom components for the enclosure. We also integrated Arduino hardware to bring the prototype to life, programming the electronics so that every interaction functioned as intended. This combination of digital UX design and physical computing gave me valuable insight into designing products that bridge both software and hardware.

Working in a multidisciplinary team taught me how important collaboration and communication are throughout the design process. We regularly presented our ideas, gathered feedback from classmates and instructors, and continuously improved the product based on testing results. This iterative approach allowed us to create a solution that better matched the needs and expectations of our target audience.

This project significantly strengthened my skills in user research, UX design, ideation, prototyping, usability testing, and collaborative problem-solving. It also introduced me to physical computing and hardware prototyping, giving me experience with Arduino programming and laser-cut manufacturing. Most importantly, I learned how valuable it is to involve users throughout the entire design process and how research-driven design decisions can lead to more meaningful, engaging, and effective products.
